\begin{abstract}
Multi-stream video inference on edge devices creates coupled GPU contention that degrades per-stream throughput as concurrent stream count grows.
We formulate this scheduling problem as a Partially Observable Markov Decision Process (POMDP) and implement Active Inference (AIF) as a training-free, belief-based scheduler on a physical three-node Jetson Orin Nano cluster with four execution modes (FULL, LITE, SKIP, OFFLOAD).
Evaluated across four workload scenarios with five independent runs, AIF achieves 86.0\% average SLO satisfaction — competitive with a threshold-based heuristic (87.8\%) and DQN (88.2\%) without any training data, and with lower cross-run variance than DQN.
A sensitivity analysis of the generative model reveals the dominant performance lever: correcting OFFLOAD likelihood semantics from per-stream to system-level improves SLO by 7.9~pp, and penalizing SKIP in the likelihood model yields a further 7.9~pp — two corrections that together outweigh all secondary hyperparameter effects combined ($<$8~pp).
The central finding is that model specification, not algorithmic complexity, is the primary challenge for AIF deployment in edge scheduling.
\end{abstract}

Active Inference, Edge Computing, Video Analytics, POMDP, Resource Scheduling, Generative Model

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Edge video analytics pipelines run multiple concurrent DNN inference streams under tight GPU memory and latency constraints~\cite{ananthanarayanan2017real}.
As stream count grows, GPU contention degrades throughput: a Jetson Orin Nano running YOLOv8n~\cite{jocher2023yolov8} drops from 10.6 to 8.7~FPS when a sixth 720p stream is added, triggering SLO violations.
A scheduler must dynamically decide, per stream and per control step, whether to process at full resolution (FULL, 640px), reduced resolution (LITE, 320px), skip frames (SKIP), or offload to a remote worker node (OFFLOAD).

This problem is structurally a POMDP: the scheduler observes only noisy per-stream FPS and latency, while the true GPU contention state — shaped by thermal throttling, queueing, and concurrent loads — is hidden.
Threshold-based heuristics react to observed violations but cannot reason under uncertainty~\cite{mao2019learning}.
RL-based schedulers can optimize long-horizon objectives but require per-scenario training, exhibit seed-dependent variance, and offer limited interpretability.

We investigate \emph{Active Inference} (AIF)~\cite{friston2010free,parr2022active} as an alternative: a Bayesian framework in which agents maintain explicit beliefs over hidden states and select actions by minimizing Expected Free Energy (EFE), jointly optimizing goal-seeking and information-seeking objectives without training.
Applying AIF to edge video scheduling reveals a consistent and actionable finding: the primary determinant of AIF performance is not the algorithmic structure, but how actions are semantically encoded in the generative model — specifically the likelihood matrices for OFFLOAD and SKIP.

\textbf{Contributions:}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=0pt]
    \item A compact POMDP formulation for multi-stream edge video scheduling, implemented on a physical three-node Jetson Orin Nano cluster with real GPU contention and network offloading.
    \item A practical AIF scheduler operating under a 1-second control loop with one-step EFE approximation, requiring no training data.
    \item An empirical demonstration that OFFLOAD and SKIP likelihood semantics each contribute $+$7.9~pp to SLO compliance, dominating all secondary hyperparameters combined ($<$8~pp), and identifying model specification as the key deployment challenge.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Related work.}
Multi-stream video scheduling has been addressed through model selection~\cite{jiang2018chameleon}, resolution adaptation~\cite{zhang2017live}, and DNN offloading~\cite{kang2017neurosurgeon}, but primarily under full observability.
AIF has been applied to computing continuum orchestration~\cite{sedlak2024equilibrium} and adaptive edge stream processing~\cite{sedlak2024stream}; the latter is closest to our work but addresses generic stream tasks rather than video-specific execution modes and coupled GPU contention.
To our knowledge, this is the first study to characterize how the \emph{semantics} of offloading and skip actions in the AIF generative model determine scheduler performance.

\section{System and Problem Formulation}
\label{sec:problem}

\subsection{Testbed and Execution Modes}

Our testbed is a three-node edge cluster of identical Jetson Orin Nano 8GB devices (1024 CUDA cores, JetPack R36.5, Gigabit Ethernet) arranged in a role-asymmetric topology (\Cref{fig:architecture}): one \emph{coordinator} runs the controller and local GPU inference; two \emph{worker nodes} serve as stateless HTTP inference servers.
All video streams use YOLOv8n on 720p input.

Profiling reveals the SLO boundary (FPS $\geq 10$, P95 $\leq 150$~ms): FULL mode (640px) supports up to 5 concurrent local streams; LITE (320px) extends this to 7 streams at the cost of reduced detection count; beyond 6 FULL streams, SLO violations are unavoidable without mode adaptation.
Each stream is assigned one of four modes per 1-second control step:
\textbf{FULL} — local inference at 640$\times$640 (highest quality, highest GPU cost);
\textbf{LITE} — local inference at 320$\times$320 (extends SLO boundary);
\textbf{SKIP} — no inference, zero GPU cost, zero detections;
\textbf{OFFLOAD} — HTTP POST to a worker node for remote inference ($\sim$7~FPS, $\sim$140~ms RTT, capped at $K_{\max}=2$ concurrent streams).

\begin{figure*}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{figures/architecture.pdf}
    \caption{System architecture. The coordinator runs the control loop and local GPU inference (FULL/LITE). Two identical worker nodes serve as stateless HTTP offload targets. The AIF controller (right) maintains a belief $Q(s)$ over hidden load states, evaluates Expected Free Energy per candidate action, and selects via precision-weighted softmax.}
    \label{fig:architecture}
\end{figure*}

\subsection{POMDP Formulation}

We model the scheduling problem as a POMDP $(\mathcal{S}, \mathcal{A}, \mathcal{O}, T, A, C)$.

\textbf{Hidden state} $s \in \{\textsc{Low}, \textsc{Med}, \textsc{High}\}$ abstracts GPU load by the number of locally-inferring streams $n_{\text{infer}}$:
$\textsc{Low}$: $n \leq 3$ (all SLOs satisfied);
$\textsc{Med}$: $n \in \{4,5\}$ (near boundary);
$\textsc{High}$: $n \geq 6$ (violations without adaptation).
The state is hidden because noisy FPS/latency observations are confounded by thermal throttling, queueing, and measurement noise.

\textbf{Observations} per control window are categorized as $\textsc{Good}$ (FPS $\geq 13$, P95 $\leq 100$~ms), $\textsc{Marginal}$ (FPS $\geq 10$, P95 $\leq 150$~ms), or $\textsc{Bad}$ (otherwise).
Only FULL and LITE streams contribute to the observation; SKIP and OFFLOAD streams are excluded as they do not reflect local GPU state.

\textbf{Actions} are single-stream mode changes or no-op, yielding $|\mathcal{A}| = 4N + 1$ candidates ($N$ active streams).
Constraints enforce skip ratio $\leq 30\%$, OFFLOAD $\leq K_{\max}=2$, and minimum dwell times to prevent oscillation.

\textbf{Objective:}
\begin{equation}
    \text{SLO\%} = 1 - \frac{\sum_{t,i} \mathbf{1}[\text{FPS}_{t,i} < 10 \;\lor\; \text{P95}_{t,i} > 150\text{ms}]}{\sum_{t,i} \mathbf{1}[m_{t,i} \neq \textsc{Skip}]}
    \label{eq:slo}
\end{equation}

\section{AIF-Based Scheduler}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Generative Model}

The AIF generative model comprises four components.

\textbf{Likelihood $A$: $P(o \mid s, m)$.}
For each mode $m$, a $3{\times}3$ matrix specifies the probability of observing each category given each load state.
FULL and LITE likelihoods are derived directly from hardware profiling:
\begin{equation}
    A^{\textsc{Full}} = \begin{bmatrix}
        0.95 & 0.04 & 0.01 \\
        0.25 & 0.50 & 0.25 \\
        0.02 & 0.13 & 0.85
    \end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}
where rows are load states (\textsc{Low}, \textsc{Med}, \textsc{High}) and columns are observation categories (\textsc{Good}, \textsc{Marginal}, \textsc{Bad}).

\textbf{OFFLOAD likelihood: system-level semantics.}
A naïve design encodes OFFLOAD using the offloaded stream's own poor metrics ($\sim$7~FPS, near-BAD for all states), causing the controller to systematically avoid OFFLOAD.
The correct design models OFFLOAD's \emph{system-level effect}: offloading frees local GPU capacity, improving the remaining streams.
This benefit is largest at HIGH load (removing one of 6+ streams significantly reduces GPU contention):
\begin{equation}
    A^{\textsc{Offload}}_{\text{v1}} = \begin{bmatrix}
        0.30 & 0.55 & 0.15 \\
        0.50 & 0.35 & 0.15 \\
        0.60 & 0.30 & 0.10
    \end{bmatrix}
    \label{eq:offload_v1}
\end{equation}
This design decision is the paper's central finding, validated empirically in \Cref{sec:ablation}.

\textbf{SKIP likelihood.}
The default $A^{\textsc{Skip}} = [0.50, 0.50, 0.00]$ treats frame-skipping as observationally neutral, making SKIP appear costless in EFE computation.
Setting $A^{\textsc{Skip}} = [0.20, 0.45, 0.35]$ (moderate penalty) encodes SKIP as a degraded-service action with real cost, which substantially reduces over-use of SKIP.

\textbf{Transition $B$: $T(s' \mid s, a)$.}
Actions are classified as \emph{demote} (reduces local GPU load: $*\to$LITE, $*\to$SKIP, $*\to$OFFLOAD) or \emph{promote}, with soft transitions (demote: 0.75 probability of downward state shift; no-op: 0.9 self-transition).

\textbf{Preference $C$} encodes the SLO target: $C = [0.85, 0.12, 0.03]$ (strongly prefers GOOD observations).
\textbf{Prior $D$} is uniform: $D = [1/3, 1/3, 1/3]$.

\subsection{Belief Update and Action Selection}

At each control step, the controller performs a Bayesian belief update:
\begin{equation}
    Q(s)_t \propto A^{m_{\text{rep}}}_{s,\, o_t} \cdot Q(s)_{t-1}
    \label{eq:belief}
\end{equation}
where $m_{\text{rep}}$ is the representative mode of locally-inferring streams.
Actions are evaluated by Expected Free Energy:
\begin{equation}
    G(a) = \underbrace{-\mathbb{E}_{Q(o|a)}[\ln C(o)]}_{\text{pragmatic}} \;-\; \underbrace{w_e \cdot \mathbb{E}_{Q(o|a)}[\mathrm{KL}[Q(s|o,a) \| Q(s|a)]]}_{\text{epistemic}}
    \label{eq:efe}
\end{equation}
with $w_e = 0.3$.
Action selection uses precision-weighted softmax with $\beta = 4.0$.

\textbf{Engineering approximation.}
We use a one-step ($T=1$) EFE, foregoing multi-step policy sequences~\cite{parr2022active}.
This is a deliberate engineering trade-off, consistent with single-step AIF precedents in physical control~\cite{friston2016robot}: the computation takes $<$1~ms per step on the Jetson Orin Nano.
The belief $Q(s)_t$ carries forward historical information, so the controller is not fully memoryless.
A 3-step global cooldown further prevents oscillatory switching.

\section{Evaluation}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Setup}

\textbf{Scenarios.}
Four workload patterns stress different adaptation capabilities:
(1)~\emph{Ramp-up} (60~s): streams added one-by-one from 3 to 7;
(2)~\emph{Burst} (60~s): stable 4-stream load, then 3 streams added at $t=15$~s and removed at $t=35$~s;
(3)~\emph{Steady overload} (90~s): 6 streams sustained throughout;
(4)~\emph{Oscillating} (120~s): three successive add/remove waves.

\textbf{Baselines.}
All controllers share the same observation interface, action space, and constraints.
\emph{No-Op} never changes modes (lower bound).
\emph{Heuristic} applies threshold-based demotion with hysteresis (stateless, reactive).
\emph{Myopic Greedy} selects the action with the best predicted immediate SLO outcome using the same profiling table as AIF (approximate oracle upper bound).
\emph{DQN} uses a 3-layer MLP with $\epsilon$-greedy exploration, retrained from scratch each run (5 episodes per scenario); its reward penalizes SLO violations, SKIP ($-0.5$), and OFFLOAD ($-0.2$).

\textbf{Protocol.}
Five independent runs per controller$\times$scenario. DQN is retrained each run; all other controllers use fixed parameters. We report mean $\pm$ std.

\Cref{tab:main_results} presents aggregate SLO satisfaction across all runs, and \Cref{fig:four_scenario} visualizes per-step FPS behavior with 5-run confidence bands.

The overall ranking is Myopic (89.9\%) $>$ DQN (88.2\%) $>$ Heuristic (87.8\%) $>$ AIF (86.0\%) $\gg$ No-Op (62.2\%).
Default AIF is competitive with both trained (DQN) and untrained (Heuristic) baselines without any training data, and at substantially lower cross-run variance than DQN (AIF std: 1.4--4.5\%; DQN burst std: 9.7\%, range 64.6\%--94.3\% across 8 confirmed runs).

AIF is strongest in \emph{steady overload} (92.1\% $>$ Heuristic 90.9\%), where its system-level OFFLOAD model drives aggressive and stable cluster-level resource utilization (offload ratio 22.8\%).
Its weakest result is \emph{burst} (77.7\%, $-9.9$~pp vs.\ Heuristic), where the 3-step global cooldown delays reaction to sudden load spikes — an expected consequence of the T=1 approximation and the conservative stability mechanism.

DQN's burst variance reveals a structural instability: switch counts range from 3 to 87 across 8 runs, and burst SLO spans 64.6\%--94.3\%, confirming that 5-episode training produces unpredictable policies.

\section{Likelihood Sensitivity Analysis}
\label{sec:ablation}

We ablate the two likelihood matrices that most directly encode action semantics.
All results are 3-run means; secondary hyperparameters ($\beta$, $w_e$, $\tau_c$) are held at default values.
\Cref{tab:likelihood_ablation} shows the cumulative effect of correcting OFFLOAD semantics (v0$\to$v1) and penalizing SKIP.

\begin{table}[t]
    \centering
    \caption{Isolated effect of each likelihood correction (3-run mean). $\Delta$ measures improvement from that correction's own baseline.}
    \label{tab:likelihood_ablation}
    \small
    \begin{tabular}{@{}lcccc@{}}
        \toprule
        Correction & Baseline & After & $\Delta$ & Skip\% \\
        \midrule
        OFFLOAD v0$\to$v1 & 78.8 & 86.7 & \textbf{+7.9} & 13.1 \\
        SKIP neutral$\to$moderate & 85.3 & 93.2 & \textbf{+7.9} & 4.7 \\
        \midrule
        Both corrections (v1 + moderate) & 78.8 & \textbf{93.2} & \textbf{+14.4} & \textbf{4.7} \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
\end{table}

\textbf{OFFLOAD likelihood.}
The v0 likelihood encodes the offloaded stream's own poor metrics ($\sim$7~FPS, near-BAD for all states), causing the controller to assign near-zero probability to GOOD observations after offloading and consequently avoid OFFLOAD entirely (0\% offload ratio in 3 of 4 scenarios).
Correcting to the system-level v1 encoding (\Cref{eq:offload_v1}) improves average SLO by 7.9~pp and raises the offload ratio to 17--24\%, demonstrating that the controller now correctly identifies offloading as beneficial under high load.
The improvement is largest in ramp-up ($+$15.7~pp), where proactive offloading prevents SLO violations as streams accumulate.

\textbf{SKIP likelihood.}
The default neutral SKIP likelihood ($[0.50, 0.50, 0.00]$) treats frame-skipping as costless in EFE computation, causing over-use (13.1\% skip ratio) that reduces detection coverage.
The moderate penalty variant ($[0.20, 0.45, 0.35]$) encodes SKIP as a degraded-service action, reducing the skip ratio to 4.7\% and raising SLO by 7.9~pp.
Mode switch counts remain unchanged across SKIP variants ($\sim$16--36/episode), confirming the improvement reflects decision \emph{quality}, not switching behavior.

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/fig_skip_likelihood.pdf}
    \caption{SKIP likelihood tuning across scenarios. Left: SLO satisfaction increases monotonically from neutral to moderate penalty. Right: skip ratio decreases correspondingly. The two corrections together (v1 OFFLOAD + moderate SKIP) reach 93.2\%, surpassing all baselines including Myopic Greedy.}
    \label{fig:skip_ablation}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Ablation hierarchy.}
The two likelihood corrections together account for $\sim$16~pp of improvement.
By comparison, secondary hyperparameters contribute: precision $\beta$ (range 3.6~pp), epistemic weight $w_e$ (2.7~pp), cooldown $\tau_c$ (1.5~pp) — combined $<$8~pp.
This ordering demonstrates that model construction, not algorithmic tuning, is the dominant challenge for AIF deployment in this setting.

\section{Discussion and Conclusion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\textbf{When AIF helps.}
AIF provides three concrete advantages over baselines: (1)~zero-shot deployment with no training data and stable cross-run behavior; (2)~structured cluster-level decision-making — the system-level OFFLOAD likelihood drives higher offload utilization (22.8\% in steady overload) than threshold-based control; (3)~interpretability — the belief state $Q(s)$ and per-action EFE decomposition can be inspected at each step to understand \emph{why} an action was chosen.

\textbf{Belief dynamics.}
\Cref{fig:slo_bar} provides a visual overview of per-scenario results.
In steady overload, $p_{\textsc{High}}$ exceeds 0.8 within 3--5 steps, after which the controller consistently selects OFFLOAD — proactive resource management that precedes visible SLO violations rather than reacting to them.

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/fig_slo_bar.pdf}
    \caption{SLO satisfaction rate across all controllers and scenarios (mean $\pm$ std, $N=5$ runs). Error bars reveal DQN's high cross-run variance, especially in Burst. AIF achieves competitive average SLO without training data and with consistently low variance.}
    \label{fig:slo_bar}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Limitations.}
The T=1 approximation limits burst recovery: accumulating evidence over 2--3 steps before acting is a direct cost of the single-step horizon.
The generative model is hand-specified from static profiling, and cannot adapt to dynamics absent from that data (thermal throttling, network congestion under concurrent offload).

\textbf{Conclusion.}
We formulated multi-stream edge video scheduling as a POMDP and deployed AIF as a training-free belief-based controller on a physical Jetson Orin Nano cluster.
Default AIF achieves 86.0\% average SLO — competitive with heuristic and RL baselines without training — but the ablation hierarchy is the paper's central finding: correcting OFFLOAD and SKIP likelihood semantics contributes $\sim$16~pp combined, far exceeding all secondary hyperparameter effects ($<$8~pp).
This shows that for model-based schedulers under partial observability, \emph{how actions are encoded in the generative model} is more consequential than algorithmic sophistication.

The open question this work raises: \emph{can the agent learn its own action semantics?}
Treating likelihood matrices as latent variables subject to Bayesian inference — parameter learning in the full AIF framework — would allow the scheduler to build its causal understanding of OFFLOAD and SKIP effects through experience, without relying on static profiling.
This is the primary direction for follow-on work.
